Water Technology & Innovation

How science and engineering are creating new solutions to the global water crisis.

Contents

1. Overview

Technology and innovation are playing an increasingly vital role in addressing the global water crisis. From low-cost filtration systems that can be built in rural villages to satellite-based water monitoring, engineers and scientists are developing creative solutions to deliver clean water to those who need it most.

Innovation in the water sector spans a wide spectrum — from simple, low-tech solutions that can be deployed cheaply in low-income settings, to sophisticated digital infrastructure that optimises water distribution in major cities. Both ends of the spectrum are essential to achieving SDG 6.

The global water technology market is projected to exceed $1 trillion by 2030 as demand for clean water solutions grows.

2. Water Purification Technology

Ceramic Filtration

Simple ceramic pot filters can remove up to 99.9% of bacteria and protozoa from water. They are inexpensive, can be made locally, and require no electricity — making them ideal for rural communities in developing countries.

Reverse Osmosis

Reverse osmosis (RO) systems force water through semi-permeable membranes to remove contaminants including heavy metals, bacteria, and dissolved solids. Costs have dropped significantly, making RO viable for household use in many regions.

UV Disinfection

Ultraviolet light kills bacteria, viruses, and protozoa in water without the use of chemicals. Solar-powered UV systems can treat water in off-grid communities using only sunlight.

Biosand Filters

Biosand filters use layers of sand and gravel, plus a biological layer of microorganisms, to remove pathogens from water. They are durable, low-cost, and can be made from locally available materials.

3. Solar & Renewable Energy Solutions

One of the biggest barriers to clean water access in off-grid communities is energy. Traditional pumps and treatment systems require electricity that is unavailable or unaffordable. Solar-powered solutions are changing this.

  • Solar water pumps can lift water from boreholes without electricity or diesel, cutting costs and carbon emissions.
  • Solar stills use sunlight to evaporate and condense water, removing salt and contaminants — a form of solar desalination.
  • Atmospheric water generators extract moisture directly from the air using solar power, creating water from humidity in arid regions.

4. Smart Water Systems

In cities around the world, digital technology is being used to make water distribution smarter, more efficient, and more resilient.

IoT Sensors

Internet of Things (IoT) sensors embedded in pipes and reservoirs can detect leaks, monitor water quality in real time, and alert engineers to problems before they become crises — reducing water loss significantly.

AI-Powered Forecasting

Artificial intelligence is being used to predict water demand, optimise irrigation in agriculture, and forecast drought and flood events — helping governments and utilities plan ahead.

Blockchain for Water Rights

In some regions, blockchain technology is being explored to create transparent, tamper-proof records of water rights and usage, reducing disputes and corruption in water allocation.

5. The Future of Water Technology

The next decade will see transformative advances in water technology. Key areas to watch include:

  • Desalination: New membrane technologies and energy recovery systems are making seawater desalination increasingly affordable and less energy-intensive.
  • Nanotechnology: Nanomaterials can filter out contaminants at the molecular level, offering new possibilities for water purification.
  • Wastewater recycling: Advanced treatment now makes it possible to purify wastewater to drinking water standards — a circular approach already used in Singapore and parts of the USA.
  • Drone monitoring: Drones equipped with sensors can survey river basins, detect pollution sources, and monitor water levels across vast areas.
