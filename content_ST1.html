The Global Water Crisis

Understanding the scale of the problem — and what we can do about it.

Contents

  2. Causes
  3. Human Impact
  4. Environmental Impact
  5. What You Can Do

1. Overview

Water is the most fundamental resource on Earth, yet billions of people live without reliable access to safe drinking water. The global water crisis is one of the defining challenges of the 21st century, affecting health, education, gender equality, and economic development across the world.

According to the United Nations, over 2.2 billion people currently lack access to safely managed drinking water. A further 3.5 billion people do not have access to safely managed sanitation services. These numbers are not just statistics — they represent lives cut short, children kept out of school, and communities trapped in cycles of poverty.

SDG 6 calls for universal access to safe and affordable drinking water and sanitation for all by 2030. We are currently far off track.

2. Causes of the Water Crisis

The water crisis is not caused by a single factor — it is driven by a combination of environmental, political, and social pressures that vary widely between regions.

Climate Change

Rising global temperatures are disrupting the water cycle, making rainfall less predictable and droughts more severe. Glaciers that supply freshwater to hundreds of millions of people are retreating at alarming rates.

Population Growth

The global population is expected to reach nearly 10 billion by 2050. This puts enormous pressure on existing water supplies, particularly in already water-stressed regions of Sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia, and the Middle East.

Poor Infrastructure

In many low-income countries, the infrastructure needed to deliver clean water — pipes, treatment plants, storage facilities — is either absent, aging, or poorly maintained. Funding and political will to invest in water infrastructure remain major barriers.

Pollution

Industrial discharge, agricultural runoff, and improper waste disposal contaminate rivers, lakes, and groundwater supplies. Once polluted, water sources are expensive and difficult to restore.

3. Human Impact

The consequences of water insecurity fall hardest on the world's most vulnerable populations — particularly women, children, and people living in poverty.

  • Health: Drinking contaminated water causes diseases such as cholera, typhoid, and dysentery. Around 485,000 people die from diarrhoeal diseases caused by unsafe water each year.
  • Education: Girls in particular miss school due to waterborne illness or because they are responsible for collecting water for their families.
  • Gender inequality: Women and girls spend an estimated 200 million hours every day collecting water — time that could be spent in education or paid employment.
  • Economic cost: The World Bank estimates that water insecurity costs some countries up to 6% of their GDP annually through lost productivity, healthcare costs, and reduced agricultural output.

4. Environmental Impact

The water crisis does not only affect people — it has profound consequences for ecosystems and biodiversity around the world.

Freshwater ecosystems — rivers, lakes, wetlands, and aquifers — are among the most biodiverse habitats on Earth. Yet they are also the most threatened. Overextraction, pollution, and climate change have caused freshwater biodiversity to decline by an average of 84% since 1970, faster than any other ecosystem type.

Wetlands act as natural water filters and flood buffers. Over 35% of the world's wetlands have been lost since 1970. Restoring and protecting these ecosystems is one of the most cost-effective ways to safeguard freshwater supplies for future generations.

5. What You Can Do

The water crisis can feel overwhelming, but individuals — especially students — can make a real difference through everyday actions and advocacy.

  • Reduce your personal water consumption — take shorter showers and fix leaking taps.
  • Support organisations such as WaterAid, charity:water, or UNICEF's water programmes.
  • Raise awareness on social media using hashtags like #SDG6 and #WaterForAll.
  • Avoid single-use plastics that end up in waterways and oceans.
  • Eat less meat — livestock farming accounts for around 70% of global freshwater use.
  • Participate in local river or beach clean-up events.
