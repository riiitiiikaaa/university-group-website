Sanitation and Hygiene

1. Overview

Sanitation refers to the provision of facilities and services for the safe disposal of human waste. Alongside access to clean water, sanitation is one of the most powerful tools for protecting human health and dignity. Yet today, billions of people still lack access to even basic sanitation services.

SDG 6 specifically targets ending open defecation and achieving access to adequate and equitable sanitation and hygiene for all by 2030. Progress is being made, but it is not happening fast enough — particularly in rural areas and informal urban settlements.

3.6 billion people — nearly half the world's population — lack access to safely managed sanitation.

2. The Scale of the Problem

Around 419 million people still practise open defecation — relieving themselves outdoors with no toilet at all. This practice spreads disease, contaminates water sources, and poses particular risks to women and girls, who face safety concerns when going outside at night.

  • 2.3 billion people use basic sanitation services but lack safely managed facilities.
  • 673 million people still defecate in the open as of 2020.
  • Poor sanitation costs countries up to 5% of GDP through healthcare costs and lost productivity.
  • Diarrhoeal diseases linked to poor sanitation kill around 432,000 people annually.

3. Sanitation in Schools

Access to sanitation in schools is essential for keeping children — especially girls — in education. When schools lack adequate toilet facilities, girls who have reached puberty often drop out rather than deal with menstruation without private, clean facilities.

Key Facts

  • 1 in 3 schools worldwide lacks basic sanitation facilities.
  • Over 600 million school-age children attend schools without adequate toilets.
  • Girls are disproportionately affected — lack of private facilities is a major driver of school dropout.

Improving school sanitation has a multiplier effect: children who stay in school are more likely to adopt hygiene habits that they carry into adulthood, benefiting their future families and communities.

4. The Power of Handwashing

Handwashing with soap is one of the most cost-effective public health interventions in the world. It can reduce diarrhoeal diseases by up to 40% and respiratory infections by up to 23%.

Yet globally, 3 billion people lack basic handwashing facilities with soap and water at home. The COVID-19 pandemic highlighted how critical hand hygiene is — but also how many people lack the facilities to practice it.

For every £1 invested in sanitation and hygiene, the return in healthcare savings and productivity gains is estimated at £5.

5. Taking Action

Students and young people have a vital role in improving sanitation and hygiene — both locally and globally.

  • Fundraise for organisations building toilets and handwashing stations in schools (e.g. WaterAid, UNICEF).
  • Advocate for better sanitation facilities on your own campus or in your local community.
  • Share factual information about sanitation to reduce stigma and raise awareness.
  • Support menstrual hygiene initiatives that keep girls in school.
  • Volunteer with local charities focused on hygiene education.
